GLAUCON

I perceive, Socrates, that you are on your way to the festival?

SOCRATES

You are not far wrong Glaucon. There is to be a competition to see who can yell the loudest, in honor of the goddess, which will take place in the evening.

GLAUCON

Ah, a fine sport. And may we suppose, then, that the men participating in this competition will be nude, as is the custom?

SOCRATES

How strange a disposition you possess, Glaucon. It is a certainty; for how else might they compete in a manner that is fair and just?

GLAUCON

That, I suppose, was to be inferred.

SOCRATES

And what profit is there in a yelling contest if the prize is not awarded with justice?

GLAUCON

Yes, that appears to me to be the truth. But Socrates, if the content is to take place in the evening why, then, are you on your way into town now, when it is not yet midday?

SOCRATES

I confess, Glaucon, I have no other pursuit. Having set my mind upon this course, I thought it better to follow through, seeing as no other duty compelled me.

GLAUCON

It is a certainty. But perhaps, Socrates, a man in your condition might be persuaded to seek a different course? O come, sit and converse, and after we have shared a meal, we may proceed to the festival in each other's company.

SOCRATES

I will converse with you Glaucon, but I don’t believe it would be due and proper to dine with you.

GLAUCON

And why is that?

SOCRATES

You are most unlearned in the art of cookery, Glaucon. A man would sooner choose to eat earth than the food prepared by you; for the earth would offer a more congenial taste and prove less troublesome to the belly.

GLAUCON

Tinnitus partook of my table some time ago, and he affirmed that my cooking was of a good sort. He said it was well-suited to my nature.

SOCRATES

Tinnitus, after the manner of poets, is stupid in his disposition-- ooh wee ouchie!

GLAUCON

But Tinnitus is the son of Asparagus, and is counted among the statesmen of the city.

SOCRATES

Owchie! Ow! Oof! I have been bit by a bug of some kind. Ooh wee ouchie!

GLAUCON

And what of the nature of justice, Socrates? We have spoken before of horses. When they are injured, are they improved or deteriorated?

SOCRATES

Owie! Ow-wow! My mind is perplexed and unable to follow the argument! Some sort of bug has bit me right in the nipple!

GLAUCON

Do you, being a practitioner of rhetoric, hope by your art to render me improved or deteriorated?

SOCRATES

Glaucon, your capacity for observation is even more mean than your skill in cookery. I require a man who possesses the science of medicine to examine my nipple!

GLAUCON

I possess neither the science of the physician nor knowledge of the nipple, but if it pleases you, I shall contemplate the wound.

SOCRATES

Owchie! Ow-wow! The pain is as if Ares himself had set his teeth against me. It is as though a hundred warriors, well-trained in the art of the pinch, had made my nipple their target.

GLAUCON

(looking at the nipple)

Socrates, I perceive that the flesh of your breast has taken the hue of darkness. Whereas it ought to be of a shade akin to the lips, it is now as black as the shadows of the den, and this darkness increases its dominion.

SOCRATES

Glaucon-- I sense the vitality of my soul departing.

GLAUCON

Truly, your countenance is like unto ash, Socrates

SOCRATES

Some kind of bug has bit me in the nipple, and I am now to be numbered among the dead.

GLAUCON

Say not so, Socrates! You are a man of noble nature, and the State is much improved by the light of your reason.

SOCRATES

The hour of our departure is not ours to command, but is a matter for the Gods alone.

GLAUCON

Any final wisdom would you now deliver, so that I may repeat the tale to your disciples?

SOCRATES

Oh no, Glaucon, will you truly report to my disciples that their master was laid by a bug of some kind biting his nipple? I am a man of high repute and steady soul; such a ridiculous end is quite contrary to my nature.

GLAUCON

Ah, but Socrates, is not truth the very essence of virtue? You are a man of justice who has sought the ascent of the soul for all citizens. Would it not be a great injustice to your memory to obscure the truth of your passing with a fabrication?

SOCRATES

(through gritted teeth)

No! No! That ass Plato persists in recording in writing my every word! I have argued that the written word is fit only for the dull-witted, yet he continues his writing in secret. He claims his aim is to "guard my memory," but if he learns I perished from a bug of some kind biting my nipple, that tale shall be my only monument. My pursuit of the good and the true will be forgotten, and men will speak only of the bug of some kind that bit my nipple and brought my end.

GLAUCON

I see. Your reasoning is sound. By what figure, then, shall we describe your departure? What end would be most harmonious with a life of philosophy?

SOCRATES

We might claim that my intellect was so great as to surpass even Apollo, and that the god, in his envy, moved Zeus to strike me with his bolt.

GLAUCON

Not so; it is a dangerous thing to speak falsely of one's death, but to bring the immortals into our deception is a risk too great to endure.

SOCRATES

Hmm. That appears to be true.

GLAUCON

(excited)

Behold! We shall say an eagle, seeking to break the shell of a tortoise upon a stone, mistook your head for a rock and thus delivered the creature upon you, ending your life

SOCRATES

What the fuck? Why should I desire an end so strange and lacking in dignity? Keep such unusual tales for your companions among the poets and makers of images.

GLAUCON

I ask your pardon; I did not know my suggestion would so displease you. To my mind, a death from the heavens seems a most remarkable thing.

SOCRATES

Ooh-wee owie ow ow! Oh gods! I can feel myself losing consciousness. I am fast approaching the end of my course. Please do not share that foolish eagle story with Plato. Men will not speak of the eagle, they will only say a tortoise delivered my demise, which is a more miserable end than getting bit on the nipple by a bug of some kind.

GLAUCON

O wiseguy. Your notion of perishing as a martyr to reason is not without merit; it is only the inclusion of the gods that makes it ill-advised... is there any man toward whom you feel a great hatred, whom we might say you defeated in argument, leading him to take your life in his shame?

SOCRATES

I hold no man in such low regard that I would wish to accuse him of murder.

GLAUCON

But do you not often speak with bitterness of Plato?

SOCRATES

It is true I complain of him to you, and of you to him... yet neither of you is so great a burden as the Republic itself... I persist in instructing them that the city ought to be governed by a philosopher king--specifically myself--but they reject this most excellent plan and still demand my tax money.

GLAUCON

Socrates you’re a genius!

SOCRATES

I am aware; I shall be the measure of intelligence for generations to come. But tell me, why do you say this now?

GLAUCON

I shall report to all that the State was your executioner. I will say your ideas were so profound and so disruptive to the order that the statesmen felt their power to be in jeopardy and thus resolved to kill you.

SOCRATES

That is a most wise device. Let it be known that I sought to banish the poets and artists--whose works are deceptions and offer only foolishness--and that I requested the role of philosopher king for myself, and the State, being unready for such brilliant truth, put me to death.

GLAUCON

I am well pleased that we had this communion of mind.

SOCRATES

Oooh. Owww. Eeeee. I am dying. The end is come. I am now dead.

GLAUCON

Yes this appears to be true. Your confidence is preserved. You were a man of virtue. I trust that in the future, men shall construct a marvelous autonomous loom that shall fix your your likeness upon their socks. A weaving machine.

SOCRATES

I’m dead.
